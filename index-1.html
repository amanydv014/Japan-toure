Where to wander

Kyoto's
Neighborhoods

Each district carries its own mood. A morning in Gion is nothing like an evening in Fushimi — knowing which to visit when changes everything.

Gion
The Geisha Quarter

Kyoto's most storied neighbourhood. Wooden machiya townhouses line Hanamikoji-dori, and geiko still walk the lanes in the early evening. Go at dusk.

Culture Nightlife Heritage
Arashiyama
The Bamboo Hills

Bamboo groves, a monkey park, and the Tenryu-ji garden on the river's edge. Arrive at 7am before the crowds arrive — it's a different world.

Nature Temples
Fushimi
Gates & Sake District

Home to the 10,000 vermillion torii gates of Fushimi Inari and Kyoto's sake-brewing district. The upper mountain trails are eerily crowd-free.

Shrine Sake Hiking
Higashiyama
The Stone Path

A preserved 2km stone-paved path past craft shops, tea houses, and Kiyomizudera temple. The best single walk in Kyoto, any season.

Crafts Temples
Nishiki
Kyoto's Kitchen

A narrow covered market running five blocks — 100+ stalls of pickled plum, fresh tofu, grilled skewers, and green tea everything. Don't eat beforehand.

Street Food Market
Pontocho
The Hidden Alley

A lane so narrow two people can barely pass. Every door opens to a restaurant — yakitori smoke rising, paper lanterns glowing. Best discovered hungry.

3-Day
Itinerary

A realistic, walking-friendly plan. Each day has a geographic logic — you won't spend your trip on a bus.

7:30 am
Kiyomizudera Temple
Arrive before the souvenir shops open. The wooden stage extending over the forested hillside is worth the early alarm. Free entry to the grounds, ¥500 for the main hall.
Temple
9:30 am
Sannenzaka & Ninenzaka
Walk downhill through these stone-paved lanes — antique shops and ceramics studios — while the light is still soft. Stop for a matcha soft-serve.
Crafts
11:00 am
Nishiki Market
Kyoto's five-block covered food market. Graze your way through — grilled squid, pickled ginger, warm yuba (tofu skin), and chewy mochi.
Street Food
2:00 pm
Gion District Walk
Wander Hanamikoji-dori on foot. Peek into the Ichiriki Chaya teahouse gate and walk the back lanes of Shimbashi — arguably the most photogenic street in Japan.
Culture
5:00 pm
Yasaka Shrine
Free to enter and spectacular at dusk when the stone lanterns are lit. The pagoda from here looking into Higashiyama is one of Kyoto's great views.
Shrine
7:30 pm
Dinner in Pontocho
Walk the narrow alley and choose by smell and atmosphere. In summer, many restaurants open hanamizake riverside terraces over the Kamogawa — book ahead.
Dining
6:30 am
Fushimi Inari Taisha
The famous 10,000 torii gates. Walk past the first hour of tourists all the way to the summit — it gets wilder and more atmospheric the higher you go. Total loop: 2–3 hours.
Shrine
10:30 am
Sake Breweries, Fushimi
The Gekkeikan Okura Sake Museum is a five-minute walk. Tasting cup included in the ¥600 entry. The canal streets out front are quietly beautiful.
Sake
2:00 pm
Arashiyama Bamboo Grove
Take the Sagano Romantic Train from Saga-Arashiyama. The bamboo grove is a five-minute walk from the station. Go left where the path forks — far fewer people.
Nature
3:30 pm
Tenryu-ji Garden
One of Kyoto's finest Zen gardens, with the Arashiyama mountains as a borrowed backdrop. The garden path to the bamboo grove is worth the extra ¥300.
Temple
5:30 pm
Boat Ride on the Oi River
Rent a rowboat for ¥1,500/hour at the boathouse near Togetsukyo Bridge. Evening light on the river and hills is genuinely stunning.
Outdoors
7:00 pm
Tofu Kaiseki Dinner
Arashiyama is known for its yudofu (hot tofu) restaurants. Shoraian, perched over the river, is worth booking two days in advance.
Kaiseki
8:00 am
Philosopher's Path
A 2km canal walk under cherry trees (spectacular in April). Start from Nanzenji and walk north. The path earns its name — it invites slow, quiet movement.
Walk
10:00 am
Nanzenji Temple
The huge sanmon gate offers a panorama of the city treetops. The aqueduct running through the temple grounds is a peculiar and wonderful anachronism.
Temple
12:00 pm
Nishiki Lunch, Slow Walk
Return to Nishiki or try a neighbourhood soba shop. Take the afternoon with no agenda — Kyoto reveals itself when you stop trying to see everything.
Lunch
2:30 pm
Nijo Castle
The shogun's opulent Kyoto residence. The "nightingale floors" squeak deliberately — a clever alarm system. The inner chambers feel genuinely untouched.
History
5:00 pm
Tea Ceremony
Book a 45-minute ceremony at En tea house in Higashiyama — small, unhurried, and conducted entirely in the spirit of wabi-sabi. No tea experience needed.
Experience
7:30 pm
Last Night in Gion
An izakaya dinner — grilled skewers, cold Sapporo, and whatever the chef recommends. Gion Hitomi on Shijo is a local favourite that doesn't require a reservation.

Eat like a local

Kyoto's
Essential Dishes

Kyoto cuisine (kyo-ryori) is subtle, vegetable-forward, and built around the tea ceremony aesthetic. These are the things you must eat.

🍜
Ramen Kyoto-style
Rich chicken broth, thin noodles

Distinct from Tokyo or Sapporo ramen — Kyoto's version uses a dark soy-chicken broth with thin straight noodles and a generous amount of spring onion. Warmer, darker, more savoury.

Try it at: Honke Daiichiasahi, near Kyoto Station
🥢
Kaiseki
The art of the multi-course meal

The apex of Japanese cuisine: a seasonal, multi-course progression of tiny, precise dishes. Even a lunch kaiseki (¥3,000–¥6,000) will rearrange your understanding of what food can be.

Try it at: Kikunoi Honten or Nakamura in Gion
🫘
Yudofu
Hot tofu in dashi broth

Kyoto's most iconic vegetarian dish — silken tofu simmered gently in kombu dashi, served with small dipping condiments. Deceptively simple. Deeply satisfying.

Try it at: Tousuiro, near Nijo Castle
🍡
Mitarashi Dango
Sweet soy-glazed rice dumplings

Skewered glutinous rice balls basted in a sweet-salty soy caramel sauce. Originally offered at Shimogamo Shrine, they're now sold all over the city and are the perfect walking snack.

Try it at: Kamo Mitarashi Chaya, Shimogamo
🍵
Matcha Everything
The defining flavour of Kyoto

Soft-serve, cake, noodles, beer — Kyoto puts matcha in things you wouldn't expect and pulls it off. The Ippodo Tea shop near the Imperial Palace is the best place to buy it to take home.

Try it at: Tsujiri Honten, Gion or Nakamura Tokichi
🍱
Obanzai
Kyoto's home cooking tradition

Small shared plates of seasonal vegetables, pickles, and fish — the everyday counterpart to kaiseki's formality. Found in neighbourhood shokudo and depachika basement food halls.

Try it at: Izuju in Gion or Nishiki Market stalls

Plan your visit

Good to
Know

The things that save time, money, and embarrassment. Kyoto rewards preparation — here's the short version.

🚌
Getting Around

The bus network covers nearly everything. A ¥700 day pass is excellent value. IC cards (Suica/Pasmo) work on all buses and the subway. Cycling is ideal in flat central Kyoto — rentals from ¥800/day.

💴
Money & Payments

Carry cash — many temples, small restaurants, and traditional shops are cash-only. 7-Eleven and Japan Post ATMs reliably accept foreign cards. Budget ¥8,000–¥15,000/day for mid-range travel.

🙏
Temple Etiquette

Remove shoes when indicated. Don't photograph inside main halls unless signs allow it. At shrines, bow twice, clap twice, make your wish, bow once. Never touch ritual objects.

📶
Connectivity

Buy a pocket WiFi or data SIM at the airport (IIJmio, HISMobile). Google Maps works reliably. Download the Navitime Japan app as backup — it handles train changes better.

🏨
Where to Stay

Gion for atmosphere. Near Kyoto Station for convenience. Try a machiya guesthouse (restored townhouse) for one night — the experience is worth the price premium. Book 3+ months ahead for peak season.

🌸
Peak Season

Cherry blossom (late March–April) and autumn foliage (November) are spectacular but extremely crowded. Book accommodation 6 months ahead. Shoulder months — June, September — offer space and lower prices.

🗺️
Day Trips

Nara (45 min) for deer and Todai-ji. Osaka (15 min on Shinkansen) for street food and nightlife. Hiroshima + Miyajima (90 min) is a full day but worth it.

🌡️
What to Pack

Comfortable walking shoes — you'll cover 15–20km/day easily. Lightweight layers for temple visits. A small day bag. Summers are hot and humid; winters are cold but rarely icy.
